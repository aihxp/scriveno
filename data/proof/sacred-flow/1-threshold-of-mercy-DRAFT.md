# Passage 1 Draft: The Threshold Of Mercy

Mercy is sometimes mistaken for the removal of consequence. Accountability is sometimes mistaken for the absence of mercy. The passage refuses both reductions. It stands at the threshold where a person is seen truthfully and still not abandoned to the worst thing that can be said about them.

The force of mercy here is not sentimental softness. It does not make wrong action unreal. It makes return possible without pretending that nothing was broken. That distinction matters. A mercy without memory becomes denial. A memory without mercy becomes a closed door.

Accountability, in this frame, is not the opposite of compassion. It is the form compassion takes when repair must become visible. The accountable person is not merely accused. The accountable person is summoned back into truthful relation: with the divine command, with the injured neighbor, with the community, and with the self that must learn not to hide.

The passage therefore gives the reader a question rather than a slogan: what would it mean to be corrected without being discarded? That question protects the teaching from two distortions. It keeps mercy from becoming indulgence, and it keeps judgment from becoming pride.
